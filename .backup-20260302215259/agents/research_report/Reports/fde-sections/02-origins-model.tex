\section{Origins: From Forward Operating Bases to Silicon Valley}

\subsection{The Palantir Genesis (2003--2016)}

The term ``Forward Deployed Engineer'' was coined at Palantir Technologies
during its founding years serving the U.S.\ intelligence community. The
name carried deliberate military resonance: early Palantir engineers were
literally embedded at forward operating bases in conflict zones, aboard
Navy ships, and at intelligence agency facilities. The role was born from
necessity---Palantir's Gotham platform required deep integration with
classified, messy, heterogeneous data systems that could not be understood
remotely.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Internal codenames}: FDEs were known as ``Delta''
        internally; Deployment Strategists (the non-engineering
        complement) were called ``Echo.''
    \item \textbf{Scale}: Until 2016, Palantir employed \textit{more}
        FDEs than traditional software engineers---a radical inversion of
        typical engineering org structures.
    \item \textbf{Project Frontline (2015)}: Created by Vinoo Ganesh,
        this program cycled 250+ engineers through forward deployed
        rotations, building institutional muscle memory for
        customer-facing engineering.
    \item \textbf{Palantir Foundry (2016)}: The launch of Foundry marked
        a strategic pivot. Many FDE-built solutions were generalized into
        platform capabilities, and a significant cohort of FDEs
        transitioned to product engineering roles.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Crisis Response: Hurricane Sandy (2012)}

In a defining moment for the FDE model, Palantir FDEs deployed to
Far Rockaway, New York during the aftermath of Hurricane Sandy. Engineers
worked on-site in disaster conditions, applying Palantir's data
integration capabilities to emergency response coordination---
demonstrating that the ``forward deployed'' ethos extended beyond military
contexts to any environment where data complexity met human urgency.

\subsection{The Circular Flow}

The economic engine of a well-run FDE program is iterative generalization:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{FDE \#1} builds a custom solution for Customer A.
    \item \textbf{FDE \#2} encounters a similar problem at Customer B,
        reuses and adapts FDE \#1's work.
    \item \textbf{FDE \#3} refines the pattern at Customer C.
    \item After \textbf{N iterations}, the solution is generalized into a
        platform-standard capability available to all customers without
        FDE involvement.
\end{enumerate}

This circular flow is what distinguishes an FDE program from consulting:
each deployment creates durable assets that compound across the customer
base.

\subsection{Auftragstaktik: Mission-Type Tactics}

Palantir's FDE doctrine draws explicitly from \textit{Auftragstaktik},
the German military concept of mission-type tactics: leadership defines
the objective and constraints, but the FDE has total autonomy over
execution. This enables rapid decision-making in the field without
waiting for HQ approval---critical when working inside customer
environments with unique constraints and time pressure.

\subsection{Pod Model (Salesforce Variant)}

Salesforce's 2025--2026 FDE expansion introduced a structured pod model:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Composition}: 1 Deployment Strategist + 2 FDEs per pod.
    \item \textbf{Duration}: Full-time on one client for approximately
        3 months.
    \item \textbf{Focus}: Agentforce deployment and customization.
    \item \textbf{Scale target}: 1,000 FDEs planned.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{On-Site Expectations}

Across the industry, FDE on-site time typically ranges from 25--50\% of
working hours. At companies with the most intensive models (OpenAI,
Palantir), FDEs spend 3--4 days per week at customer facilities, writing
code directly on customer infrastructure.
